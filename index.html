Allan Watts

The visionary who was out to save the world
A prolific author and speaker, Alan Watts was one of the first to interpret Eastern wisdom for a Western audience.

    Alan's life is not a journey (pun intended):

  • 1915 - Alan Wilson Watts was born in the countryside near London, England.
  • 1932 - he produced his first booklet, An Outline of Zen Buddhism, a summary based on the Zen writings of D. T. Suzuki.
  • 1938 - he moved to the United States to study Zen in New York, where he soon began lecturing in bookstores and cafes and became an Episcopal priest for a time.
  • 1940 - published The Meaning of Happiness, a book based on his talks. Ironically, the book was issued on the eve of the second World War.
  • 1950 - Alan’s time as a priest had run its course, and he left the Church and Chicago for upstate New York. There he settled into a small farmhouse outside Millbrook.
  • 1951 - he moved to San Francisco where, at Dr. Frederic Spiegelberg’s invitation, he began teaching Buddhism at the American Academy of Asian Studies
  • 1953 - he accepted a Saturday evening slot on Berkeley’s KPFA radio station, then he began a broadcast series titled “The Great Books of Asia”
  • 1956 - began his popular radio show, “Way Beyond the West.”
  • By the early sixties, Alan’s radio talks aired nationally and the counterculture movement adopted him as a spiritual spokesperson.
  • By the late-sixties, he moved into a cabin on the nearby slopes of Mount Tamalpais. There he became part of the Druid Heights artist community in the late sixties.
  • 1973 - after returning from a whirlwind lecture tour that took him through the U.S., Canada, and European, Alan passed away in his sleep, on the mountain he loved.

"Perhaps the foremost interpreter of Eastern disciplines for the contemporary West, Alan Watts had the rare gift of ‘writing beautifully the un-writable’. Watts begins with scholarship and intellect and proceeds with art and eloquence to the frontiers of the spirit. A fascinating entry into the deepest ways of knowing.”
